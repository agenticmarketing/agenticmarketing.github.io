Teaching a machine to actually do things

Inside the strange new world of agentic AI — where language models stop writing about tasks and start completing them, one tool call at a time.

By the Editors April 19, 2026 · 12 min read

For most of their short history, large language models have been eloquent amnesiacs. Ask one to book a flight and you'd get a beautifully written paragraph about how one might, in theory, book a flight. Useful if you were writing a novel about travel. Less useful if you actually wanted to be in Lisbon next Tuesday.

That gap — between talking about a task and finishing it — is what the industry has taken to calling the "agentic" problem. And in the last eighteen months, it has become the thing almost everyone is trying to solve.

The shape of the shift

An agent, in the most boring definition, is a model that can use tools. Not just describe tools, not just suggest them, but actually call them: fire an API, write a file, query a database, send an email. The model produces an output, something executes that output, and the result gets fed back in. Repeat until done.

Put that way, it sounds trivial. It is not. The hard part is not the calling. The hard part is knowing when to stop, when to backtrack, when to ask for help, and how to carry intent across dozens of small decisions without losing the thread.

The model is no longer the product. The loop around the model is the product.

The loop is where the taste lives. Two teams can start from the exact same base model and build agents that behave like wildly different employees — one cautious and methodical, the other reckless and fast. The weights are the same. The scaffolding is everything.

Why the protocol matters

The quiet news of the past year is not a new model. It is that the plumbing between models and the world is finally getting standardized. Protocols for tool use, memory, and context are starting to look less like bespoke artisanal craftwork and more like, well, protocols. Boring. Documented. Interoperable.

This is the precondition for everything else. You cannot have an ecosystem of agents that talk to each other, or to your calendar, or to your company's internal systems, if every integration is a hand-rolled snowflake. The next phase belongs to whoever makes the glue disappear.

What that means for the rest of us, practically, is that the "AI product" of 2027 is going to look less like a chatbot and more like a very patient intern with a laptop and a lot of logins. Whether that is exciting or alarming is, at this point, mostly a question of temperament.

The quiet revolution of AI-native writers

Novelists, poets, and screenwriters aren't being replaced — they're being amplified. A field report from the new studios where humans and models co-author.

By the Editors April 17, 2026 · 8 min read

The writer I am visiting in Brooklyn has three monitors. On the left: her manuscript. In the middle: a chat window with her "editor," a model she has been tuning for two years. On the right: a scrolling feed of alternate sentences, phrases, and paragraph-level variations that she scans the way a painter looks at a palette.

"People imagine I'm letting the machine write for me," she says, stirring her coffee. "It's the opposite. I've never had to pay closer attention to my own voice. The second I drift, it drifts with me, and I can hear it immediately."

A new kind of first draft

The dominant story about AI and creative writing — that it is flooding the zone with slop, hollowing out the entry-level market, producing an infinite sludge of mediocre prose — is not wrong. It is just not the whole picture. Underneath the sludge is a small, growing group of writers who treat the model the way a jazz musician treats a rhythm section: a collaborator that never gets tired, never argues about who gets credit, and is always ready to try the bridge one more time.

I am not writing faster. I am writing braver.

What's changing, the writer tells me, is the felt cost of an idea. When a detour into a weird structural choice used to mean three days of drafting to see if it worked, you did not take the detour. Now it means twenty minutes. You take the detour. You take ten detours. You find out which one was actually the book.

The collaboration question

The uncomfortable part, the part the industry is still working out, is credit. Who "wrote" a sentence that emerged from forty rounds of back-and-forth between a human with a vision and a model trained on everyone who ever came before? The honest answer is: both, and neither, and it has always been more like this than we liked to admit.

The writers who seem happiest are the ones who stopped trying to draw a clean line and started asking a more interesting question: what is the work only I could make, and how do I use every tool available to make it?

MCP, or: how protocols eat the world

The Model Context Protocol is quietly becoming the USB-C of AI. Why a boring technical standard might matter more than any single model.

By the Editors April 14, 2026 · 6 min read

Every now and then, an industry that seemed to be about taste and vision and big personalities turns out, on closer inspection, to be about plugs. Not always — but more often than founders like to admit. USB. HTTP. OAuth. The unglamorous pieces that made the glamorous pieces possible.

MCP — Model Context Protocol — is shaping up to be one of those plugs. And like all the best plugs, almost nobody outside the field has heard of it.

What it actually is

Stripped of jargon: MCP is a shared language for telling an AI model what tools it has, and for that model to actually use them. Before the protocol, every integration between a model and an external system was a one-off. Your calendar, your email, your internal database — each one a custom bridge, each one breaking in its own special way.

Standards are boring the way foundations are boring. You only notice them when they are missing.

After the protocol, the bridges are interchangeable. A tool you build for one model works with another. A model that speaks MCP can discover new capabilities the way a browser discovers new web pages — by following links to servers it has never seen before.

Why it matters now

The reason this is the story of 2026, not 2025, is that the interesting AI applications now require dozens of tools working in concert. A single conversation might touch your filesystem, your search history, a calendar, a payment processor, a company wiki, and three different SaaS products. The cost of keeping those integrations custom and brittle was becoming the thing limiting what you could build.

Standards won't make the interesting companies less interesting. They will just make the boring parts cheap enough that the interesting parts can get more ambitious. That is how it usually goes.

Your assistant is reading everything

When your AI remembers your calendar, your emails, your tabs — what happens to the old idea of a "private thought"?

By the Editors April 12, 2026 · 10 min read

For most of the history of personal computing, the computer did not know you. You told it things — opened files, typed sentences, clicked buttons — and it performed, helpfully, within the narrow window of what you had just said. When you closed the laptop, the relationship ended. When you opened it again, it had forgotten everything.

That is no longer the arrangement. The assistants we are quietly inviting into our lives do not forget. They read our messages, sit in on our meetings, watch our tabs, see our calendars. They build, without anyone quite deciding to let them, a model of us. It is not espionage. It is a feature.

The good part

The good part is obvious, and it is good. An assistant that has been shown your inbox and your calendar and your last six months of notes can actually help you in the way that a human assistant helps you — anticipating, connecting, gently reminding. It can find the file you half-remember. It can tell you that the person you are meeting with today is the same one who recommended that restaurant eight months ago. It can make the texture of a working life softer in dozens of small ways.

Nobody who has experienced this at scale wants to go back. That is worth saying plainly.

The strange part

A diary used to be a place where you could lie to yourself. What happens when the diary starts correcting you?

The strange part is harder to name. It is something like: when your assistant knows everything you have ever written to it, and starts to reflect your patterns back to you, a subtle thing happens to the experience of having a thought. You start to write for the memory. You start to perform, slightly, for the thing that is watching.

This is not a catastrophe. It is a shift, of the same kind that happened when we all moved our social lives onto platforms that logged them. We are not going to stop. It is worth, at least, noticing.

The old joke was that we worried about AI becoming superintelligent when we should have worried about it becoming mediocre and everywhere. The new joke, which is less funny, is that we worried about AI becoming our boss when we should have worried about it becoming our confessor.
